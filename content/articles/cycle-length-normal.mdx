The 28-day cycle is so embedded in how menstruation is talked about that most people assume it is the standard. It is not. It is a statistical average — and not even an accurate one. The actual distribution of cycle lengths in the population is considerably wider, and what counts as normal covers a much broader range.

### What the data shows

Large population studies using prospective cycle tracking data have mapped the actual distribution of cycle lengths. The most frequently cited range for normal cycles is 21 to 35 days. But even within this range, 28 days is not the most common length — cycles of 25–30 days are the most frequently occurring cluster, with the peak somewhere around 27 days rather than exactly 28.

A large study using data from the Clue app, published in *npj Digital Medicine* in 2019 and covering over 600,000 cycles, found that fewer than 13% of cycles were exactly 28 days. More than 65% fell in the 25–30 day range, but the full range was wide. The key finding was that most people's cycles vary from month to month — a variation of seven or more days between the shortest and longest cycle in a year was common.

### Individual variation vs population averages

The more useful question is not whether your cycle matches the textbook but whether your cycle is consistent with your own baseline.

Cycle-to-cycle variation of one to three days is common and normal. Occasional longer variation — a cycle that is 32 days when you usually have 27-day cycles — is generally unremarkable, especially if it follows a stressful period or illness. What warrants attention is consistent irregularity: cycles that vary by more than seven days regularly, cycles that are consistently shorter than 21 days or longer than 35 days, or a pattern of skipped cycles without a clear cause.

### The luteal phase is the fixed part

Understanding what drives cycle length variation helps interpret it. As described in [the four phases article](/articles/four-cycle-phases), the luteal phase is almost always 12–14 days. It does not vary much. What varies is the follicular phase — the time from menstruation to ovulation.

If you had a longer-than-usual cycle, ovulation arrived later. If your cycle was short, ovulation arrived earlier. The period always comes approximately two weeks after ovulation.

### When to track and when to investigate

Tracking cycle length for three to six cycles gives you a personal baseline. Most people find their own variation is much narrower than the population range — a person with typically 26-day cycles might vary between 24 and 29 days, while someone with 32-day cycles varies between 30 and 35.

Consistent cycles outside the 21–35 day range, or highly irregular cycles without a clear lifestyle explanation, are worth raising with a GP. PCOS, thyroid dysfunction, and other hormonal conditions can present as irregular cycle length. Documentation — not just memory — is the most useful thing to bring to that conversation.
