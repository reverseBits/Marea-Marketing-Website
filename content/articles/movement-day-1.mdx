The received wisdom is clear: rest when you have your period. Rest is genuinely useful when symptoms are severe. But the blanket idea that movement makes period pain worse is not supported by evidence. For most people with manageable symptoms, gentle movement on the first day of a period is not just safe, it actively helps.

### Where period cramps come from

Menstrual cramps, primary dysmenorrhoea, are caused by prostaglandins. These are lipid compounds produced by the uterine lining as it prepares to shed. Prostaglandins trigger uterine muscle contractions strong enough to expel the lining. High prostaglandin levels are associated with more intense contractions, restricted blood flow to the uterine muscle, and greater pain sensation.

The restricted blood flow is the key piece. When a muscle contracts repeatedly and intensely without adequate oxygen, a state called ischaemia, it generates pain. The uterine muscle contracting against reduced blood supply is precisely what creates the cramping sensation. It is the same mechanism as a muscle cramp anywhere else in the body, just happening in an organ you cannot see.

### What movement does to this mechanism

Physical activity drives two changes that directly work against this process. First, it stimulates the release of endorphins, natural analgesic compounds that reduce pain signal intensity throughout the body. This effect is not metaphorical. Endorphins bind to opioid receptors and genuinely dampen how intensely pain registers.

Second, movement increases cardiovascular output and improves blood flow to peripheral and pelvic tissues. More blood reaching the uterine muscle during contractions means more oxygen delivery, which reduces the ischaemic component of cramping. Even a gentle walk is sufficient to produce measurable increases in pelvic circulation.

### What the research actually shows

Multiple randomised controlled trials have compared exercise to no exercise for primary dysmenorrhoea, and the results consistently favour movement. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 12 trials found that exercise significantly reduced menstrual pain intensity compared to sedentary control groups. A broader review covering 29 trials found benefits across yoga, aerobics, and resistance training, not one specific modality. Crucially, these effects appeared in both same-session and longer-term outcomes across cycles.

Even low-intensity activity sustained for as little as 15 minutes showed pain-reducing effects in several studies. The threshold for benefit is low. You do not need a structured gym session to get the effect.

### Types of movement worth trying

Yoga has been studied most extensively for dysmenorrhoea specifically. Child's pose, supine twists, and gentle forward folds appear to reduce pelvic tension and support parasympathetic nervous system activation, the state that counters the stress response your body can mount during painful periods. Walking is the most accessible option and has consistent evidence. Light aerobic activity at a conversational intensity works too. The common thread is that movement is gentle enough to sustain, not intense enough to add additional physiological stress.

High-intensity training on day 1, when the body is already managing an inflammatory process, is a separate question from whether movement helps at all. For most people, matching intensity to how their body actually feels that day produces better outcomes than either forcing a hard session or avoiding all activity.

### When rest is right

Severe primary dysmenorrhoea, pain that prevents normal function, does not respond to over-the-counter pain relief, or comes with vomiting or fainting, is not something to exercise through. That level of pain is a medical referral trigger. It can indicate conditions like endometriosis that warrant investigation and treatment. If that is your situation, the goal is diagnosis, not a 20-minute walk.
