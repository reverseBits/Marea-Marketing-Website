Skin behaviour through the menstrual cycle is one of the more visible expressions of the underlying hormonal changes, and one that most people intuitively notice but have rarely had explained. The breakout before your period, the brief glow around ovulation, the dry patch mid-cycle: these follow a predictable biological logic.

### Why skin looks best in the follicular phase

Estrogen is the primary driver of skin quality in the follicular phase. It stimulates collagen production, supports skin thickness and elasticity, and promotes water retention in the skin, which contributes to the dewy, plumped appearance that many people notice in the week after their period ends.

Estrogen also supports the production of hyaluronic acid, a compound that holds water in skin tissue. Higher estrogen means more hyaluronic acid synthesis, which is part of why follicular-phase skin can look more luminous without any change in skincare routine.

Sebum production is relatively lower in the follicular phase, which means pores appear smaller and skin texture is smoother.

### The ovulatory glow, and the caveat

The days around ovulation are typically the skin high point. Estrogen is at its peak, collagen production is highest, and circulation increases slightly, contributing to the flushed, clear-skinned appearance associated with this phase.

The caveat: some people experience brief mid-cycle acne or skin sensitivity at ovulation, related to the LH surge and the brief testosterone peak that accompanies it.

### Why skin breaks out before your period

This is the part of the cycle that affects most people and is most poorly explained. In the late luteal phase, progesterone dominates, and progesterone stimulates sebaceous (oil) gland activity, increasing sebum production. More sebum creates a more hospitable environment for acne-causing bacteria and increases the likelihood of pores becoming blocked.

Simultaneously, the brief testosterone-like androgenic activity that occurs in the late luteal phase further stimulates oil production. The combination of elevated sebum and androgenic activity in the days before menstruation is the mechanism behind the characteristic premenstrual breakout, typically on the chin, jawline, and lower face, which are the areas with the highest density of androgen-sensitive sebaceous glands.

### Inflammation makes it worse

The late luteal and menstrual phases involve elevated inflammatory markers as the body prepares to shed the uterine lining. Skin inflammation heightens the severity of breakouts that do develop, making late luteal acne more persistent and more tender than acne at other cycle points.

### How to adjust skincare cyclically

The evidence does not support dramatically changing your skincare routine every week. But several adjustments are worth making:

In the late luteal phase, using a lower-comedogenicity moisturiser (one less likely to block pores) and increasing the frequency of gentle exfoliation helps address the increased sebum. Spot treatments applied prophylactically to known breakout areas two to three days before a period is expected can interrupt the cycle before cysts form.

In the follicular phase, when skin is more resilient, active ingredients like retinoids and exfoliating acids are better tolerated. Introducing or increasing these in the follicular phase and reducing them in the late luteal phase prevents the irritation that can compound inflammation at an already sensitive cycle point.

<div className="label">Research finding</div>

A study measuring sebum production across the menstrual cycle found peak sebum output in the late luteal and menstrual phases, with significantly lower production in the follicular phase. Androgen receptor activity in sebaceous glands also showed cyclical variation, with highest sensitivity in the premenstrual period, confirming the biological basis of the pre-period breakout pattern.

Most people find it is remarkably consistent between cycles, which makes planning skincare interventions much more precise.
