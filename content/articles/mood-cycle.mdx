Mood variability is often treated as a personality characteristic, some people are more emotionally consistent than others. But for people who menstruate, a significant portion of mood variability has a biological architecture that repeats every cycle, predictable enough to chart once you start looking for it.

### The follicular high: estrogen and neurotransmitters

In the follicular phase, from the end of menstruation to ovulation, estrogen rises steadily. Estrogen has well-documented effects on three of the brain's most mood-relevant neurotransmitters: serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine.

Estrogen increases serotonin synthesis, upregulates serotonin receptors, and reduces the rate at which serotonin is broken down. The result is higher serotonin activity, which correlates with improved mood, reduced anxiety, and greater emotional resilience. Estrogen also supports dopamine signalling, contributing to motivation, reward sensitivity, and social drive.

The ovulatory window, around days 12–16 of a typical cycle, is often the emotional high point. Many people report feeling more socially engaged, more confident, and more emotionally resilient in this phase than at any other point in the cycle.

### The luteal shift: progesterone's double role

After ovulation, progesterone rises. Progesterone has a calming, sedating effect on the nervous system via GABA, in the mid-luteal phase, this can produce a sense of tranquillity and groundedness. Some people feel genuinely relaxed in the mid-luteal phase.

The problem comes at the end of the luteal phase, when progesterone drops sharply. The nervous system loses its GABA-mediated buffer. Simultaneously, estrogen falls from its secondary luteal peak, reducing serotonin support. The combination produces the late-luteal window that many people experience as emotionally rawer, more reactive, and harder to navigate.

### Emotional reactivity vs emotional instability

An important distinction: the increased emotional reactivity in the late luteal phase is not the same as emotional instability or irrationality. Research has found that emotional responses in this phase are proportionate, they are reactions to real things, but the threshold for emotional arousal is lower. Things that would feel manageable at other cycle points can feel more intense.

A study using functional MRI found that the amygdala (the brain region involved in emotional reactivity) showed heightened activation to emotionally neutral stimuli in the late luteal phase compared to the follicular phase, confirming that the sensitivity shift is neurological, not a matter of attitude.

### Why knowing the pattern changes how you experience it

The most consistent finding in research on mood and the menstrual cycle is that awareness of the cyclical pattern reduces its negative impact. When someone understands that their emotional reactivity is higher in a predictable window, they can apply that understanding in real time: recognising that a feeling is partly cycle-driven does not make it less real, but it does shift the relationship to it.

Over several tracked cycles, most people can identify their personal pattern, which does not necessarily match the textbook timeline, and use that to contextualise what they are experiencing.

An fMRI study published in *Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience* found significantly greater amygdala activation to emotionally neutral images in the late luteal phase compared to the mid-follicular phase. Participants showed no difference in subjective ratings of the images, they did not consciously perceive them as more emotional, but their neural processing was measurably more reactive, confirming that the late luteal phase involves altered emotional processing at the neurological level.

<div className="label">Map your own pattern</div>

For two cycles, rate your mood each evening on a simple 1–5 scale in Marea. At the end of each cycle, look at the graph. Most people see a clear architecture emerge, a high window, a stable middle, and a lower window. Once you can see your own pattern, you can plan around it rather than being surprised by it.
