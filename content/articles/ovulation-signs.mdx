Ovulation is the event that the entire follicular phase builds toward, and the body signals it through several measurable changes. Most people who menstruate were never taught what these signals look like, which means an event that happens every month goes largely unnoticed.

### 1. Cervical mucus becomes clear and stretchy

This is the most reliable and consistent sign. As estrogen rises in the lead-up to ovulation, the cervix produces more mucus, and its character changes. In the days around ovulation, discharge becomes clear, slippery, and stretchy: often described as resembling raw egg white. It can stretch between the fingers without breaking.

This type of mucus is physically different from the drier, crumbly, or creamy mucus of other cycle phases. It creates a hospitable environment for sperm, providing a fluid medium they can travel through. The Billings Ovulation Method and other fertility awareness approaches use this as a primary indicator for the fertile window.

Outside of conception planning, it is a useful real-time signal of where you are in your cycle.

### 2. A brief rise in basal body temperature

Basal body temperature (BBT), your temperature at complete rest, ideally measured immediately on waking before getting up, rises by 0.2–0.5°C after ovulation. This rise is caused by progesterone, which has a thermogenic effect, and it persists throughout the luteal phase.

The rise happens after ovulation, not before, so it confirms rather than predicts it. However, over several tracked cycles, the pattern of when the rise occurs becomes predictable. BBT tracking requires a thermometer accurate to 0.1°C and consistent measurement conditions (same time each morning, before getting out of bed).

### 3. A brief one-sided ache (mittelschmerz)

Mittelschmerz, German for "middle pain", is a mild to moderate ache on one side of the lower abdomen that some people feel around ovulation. It corresponds to the follicle rupturing as it releases the egg, and to the small amount of fluid and blood that enters the pelvic cavity as this happens.

Not everyone feels it, and the side can alternate between cycles (left ovary one month, right the next). When it does occur, it tends to last from a few minutes to a few hours, occasionally a day. It is usually mild and does not require treatment.

### 4. Increased libido

Libido naturally peaks around ovulation in most people. This is biologically logical, the fertile window is the only time conception is possible. Estrogen and testosterone both peak around ovulation, and both are associated with increased sexual interest.

This shift can be subtle or pronounced and varies considerably between people and between cycles. Noticing a consistent pattern of increased sexual interest at a particular point in the cycle is often an indirect signal of ovulation timing.

### 5. Breast tenderness or sensitivity

Some people experience mild breast sensitivity around ovulation, distinct from the more pronounced premenstrual tenderness that can occur in the late luteal phase. The mid-cycle tenderness tends to be milder and briefer.

### 6. A brief energy and mood peak

Many people notice their highest energy and most positive mood in the two to three days around ovulation. This corresponds to the estrogen peak that precedes the LH surge. Estrogen supports serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine activity, all of which contribute to motivation, social energy, and general sense of wellbeing.

This is often the point in the cycle where concentration is sharpest and social energy is highest.

<div className="label">On tracking accuracy</div>

Cervical mucus observation correctly identified the fertile window in 93% of cycles in a large prospective study of women using the Billings method. BBT alone is less reliable as a predictor (it confirms ovulation after the fact) but becomes more useful when combined with mucus observation. Together, the two methods provide both a prediction window and a confirmation signal.

<div className="label">Start with one sign</div>

Rather than tracking everything at once, choose one sign, cervical mucus is the most accessible for most people, and observe it daily for one cycle. Note it in Marea alongside your cycle day. The pattern across a single cycle is usually enough to see the mid-cycle shift clearly.
