Exercise science has historically studied men or mixed cohorts where cycle phase was not controlled for. This has left a significant gap in practical knowledge for people who menstruate: how does the cycle actually affect training, recovery, and injury risk — and what should you do differently?

### The estrogen effect on strength and injury

Estrogen has significant effects on the musculoskeletal system. It increases muscle protein synthesis, supports neuromuscular coordination, and affects the mechanical properties of tendons and ligaments. In the follicular phase, as estrogen rises, muscle strength and power output measurably increase in research settings.

The same estrogen-driven changes also affect ligament laxity — the looseness of ligaments. Research on ACL (anterior cruciate ligament) injuries has found significantly higher injury rates in the ovulatory phase when estrogen peaks, as the ligament is more extensible. Athletes in high-ACL-risk sports (football, basketball, skiing) show measurably elevated injury risk in this phase. Neuromuscular training and controlled warm-ups become more important around ovulation.

### Phase-by-phase training guide

**Menstrual phase:** Energy is typically lower and the body is managing an inflammatory process. Low-to-moderate intensity movement — walking, yoga, light swimming — supports blood flow and endorphin release without adding physiological stress. For most people, this is not a phase for personal bests.

**Follicular phase:** As estrogen rises post-menstruation, energy and strength increase. This is typically the best phase for high-intensity training, strength work, and attempting new performance targets. Muscle recovery after intense sessions is faster in the follicular phase.

**Ovulatory phase:** Energy peaks but injury risk is elevated. This is a good time for skill work, team sport, and performance events — but warm-up quality and neuromuscular control matter more here. Reduce heavy unilateral leg work if injury risk is a concern.

**Luteal phase:** The early luteal phase can sustain high-intensity training, though recovery begins to slow. Core temperature is elevated (progesterone's thermogenic effect), which means higher perceived exertion in the heat. Late luteal phase — the week before the period — is where reducing intensity and prioritising recovery produces the best downstream results.

### Why rest in the late luteal phase is not laziness

The late luteal phase is characterised by elevated cortisol sensitivity, reduced sleep quality, increased body temperature, and higher baseline inflammatory markers. Training hard during this phase adds physiological stress on top of an already higher-stress hormonal environment. Recovery takes measurably longer.

The counterintuitive finding from exercise research is that athletes who reduce intensity in the late luteal phase and use it for recovery often perform better in the subsequent follicular phase — the high-output phase — than those who maintain the same volume throughout the month.

### Fuelling considerations

Carbohydrate oxidation is higher in the follicular phase; fat oxidation is higher in the luteal phase. This means pre-exercise carbohydrate loading is more useful in the follicular phase, while the luteal phase suits slightly higher fat intake relative to carbohydrate. Protein requirements increase across the board when training volume is high, and are not strongly cycle-dependent.

<div className="label">Research finding</div>

A study in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* measured peak force output across cycle phases and found significantly higher values in the follicular and ovulatory phases compared to the mid and late luteal phase. Muscle fibre recruitment patterns also differed, with the follicular phase showing more efficient neuromuscular activation — confirming that training adaptation is not uniform across the cycle.

<div className="takeaway-box">
<div className="label">One week experiment</div>

In your next luteal phase, reduce training intensity by 20–30% in the final week before your period. Note energy, mood, and sleep quality. Then track your first week of the follicular phase. Many people find this approach produces a noticeable difference in how the high-energy phase feels — and performs.
</div>
