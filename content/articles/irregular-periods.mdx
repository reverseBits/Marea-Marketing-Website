Irregular periods are one of the most common reasons people seek medical advice about their reproductive health — and also one of the most overmedicated. Many people are given hormonal contraception as a treatment for irregular cycles without an investigation into the underlying cause. Understanding what actually constitutes clinically significant irregularity, and what does not, helps you have more informed conversations with healthcare providers.

### What irregular actually means

A cycle is considered irregular if it consistently falls outside the 21–35 day range, or if the variation between the shortest and longest cycle in a given period exceeds seven days regularly. Occasional variation — a single longer or shorter cycle following a stressful month, illness, or major life change — is normal and does not meet the clinical threshold for irregular cycles.

The most important variable is your own baseline. Someone with typically 32-day cycles who occasionally has a 35-day cycle is not irregular. Someone with 27-day cycles who unpredictably varies between 22 and 38 days is.

### Causes that often resolve on their own

Stress-related cycle disruption is the most common cause of temporary irregularity. As described elsewhere, significant psychological stress suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis, delaying ovulation. Once the stress resolves, the cycle typically normalises within one to two cycles.

Extreme exercise load, significant caloric restriction, rapid weight change, or major illness can all disrupt ovulation temporarily. Cycles that were regular before the change and become irregular during it — then return to regularity as the trigger resolves — generally do not need investigation.

Recent stopping of hormonal contraception (the pill, the patch, the ring) can produce irregular cycles for several months while the HPO axis reactivates. This is normal and expected.

### Causes worth investigating

**PCOS** (polycystic ovary syndrome) is the most common hormonal cause of irregular cycles, affecting 8–13% of the population. It involves elevated androgens, disrupted insulin signalling, and often — though not always — irregular or absent ovulation. Other signs include excess hair growth (particularly on the face or body in a male-pattern distribution), persistent acne, and difficulty losing weight. Diagnosis requires clinical assessment including hormone blood tests and often pelvic ultrasound. The triad of symptoms, blood markers, and ultrasound appearance is used for diagnosis.

**Thyroid dysfunction** — both hypothyroidism (underactive) and hyperthyroidism (overactive) — disrupts the HPO axis and can produce irregular cycles. Thyroid conditions are common and often missed. A simple blood test (TSH, free T4) is sufficient to screen.

**Hyperprolactinaemia** — elevated prolactin, the hormone responsible for breast milk production — suppresses ovulation and can cause irregular or absent periods. It can be caused by a benign pituitary tumour (prolactinoma), certain medications (including some antidepressants and antipsychotics), or other factors. A blood prolactin level identifies it.

**Perimenopause** begins earlier than most people expect — sometimes in the early to mid-forties — and one of its first signs is cycle irregularity.

### What to bring to your GP

A documented cycle log — not recalled from memory — is the most useful thing to have when discussing irregular cycles with a doctor. Three to six months of Marea data, showing your cycle start dates, length variation, and any associated symptoms, gives a healthcare provider the pattern they need to make a clinical assessment.

Population data suggest that 14–25% of people who menstruate have irregular cycles at any given time. A systematic review of cycle irregularity causes found PCOS responsible in approximately 70–80% of cases where anovulation (absent ovulation) was identified as the mechanism. However, the review also noted significant under-investigation: many people with irregular cycles receive hormonal contraception as a first-line response without blood work or further assessment.

<div className="takeaway-box">
<div className="label">The documentation question</div>

Before any GP appointment for irregular cycles, have at least three months of cycle start dates recorded. If you have other symptoms — excess hair growth, acne, fatigue, cold intolerance, unexplained weight changes — note those too. Asking specifically for TSH, FSH, LH, prolactin, and testosterone blood tests gives you a comprehensive hormonal screen that most GPs will perform if requested.
