Vaginal discharge is something most people are aware of but few have been given accurate information about. School health education, if it covers discharge at all, typically limits itself to describing what is abnormal. What is less commonly taught is that normal discharge changes in a predictable pattern throughout the cycle — and that pattern carries real information.

### Why discharge changes through the cycle

The cervix produces mucus continuously. The character of this mucus — its volume, consistency, colour, and stretchiness — changes in direct response to estrogen and progesterone levels. This makes cervical mucus one of the most accessible real-time indicators of cycle phase.

The hormonal pattern is consistent: as estrogen rises in the follicular phase, mucus production increases and becomes more fluid and stretchy. It peaks around ovulation, then changes character when progesterone rises in the luteal phase, becoming thicker and less prominent. This shift was codified into fertility awareness methods in the 1970s and has been researched extensively since.

### What normal discharge looks like at each phase

**Just after menstruation:** Relatively dry. Little to no visible discharge for a few days, or a small amount of sticky, whitish mucus.

**Mid-follicular phase:** Mucus increases and becomes creamier — white or yellowish, with a lotion-like consistency.

**Approaching ovulation:** Discharge becomes clearer, wetter, and progressively more stretchy. The classic description is raw egg white — clear, slippery, and able to stretch between fingers without breaking. This is the most fertile mucus type.

**Ovulation day:** Peak wetness. Egg-white discharge at its most abundant.

**Luteal phase:** Following ovulation and the rise in progesterone, discharge becomes thicker again — white or off-white, sticky or paste-like — and decreases in volume. Many people notice a return to dryness or minimal discharge in the second half of the cycle.

### What discharge can signal

The cyclical pattern above is the baseline. Variation from this pattern can indicate several things. A consistently absent or scant mucus pattern may suggest low estrogen. An unusually short or absent egg-white phase may indicate anovulation (a cycle without ovulation). Very sparse mucus can also be a side effect of certain antihistamines or decongestants, which have drying effects.

Colour changes that indicate something worth investigating: yellow or green discharge (especially with odour or irritation), grey discharge, any discharge accompanied by significant itching, burning, or an unusual smell. These suggest bacterial vaginosis, thrush, or infection rather than normal cycle variation.

### The important normalisation

Discharge in the first half of the cycle, particularly around ovulation, can be substantial. Many people are concerned or embarrassed by the increase in discharge in the days before ovulation. It is entirely normal and physiologically useful — the cervical mucus around ovulation creates an environment that supports sperm survival and transport, serving its biological function whether conception is the goal or not.

Knowing that the wetness and egg-white consistency is a signal of the fertile window rather than something wrong changes how it is experienced.

<div className="label">Research finding</div>

A prospective study following 696 women using the Billings Ovulation Method found that correct identification of the fertile window based on mucus observation was achieved in 93% of monitored cycles. A systematic review of fertility awareness methods found cervical mucus observation to be the most effective single biological indicator for identifying ovulation timing.

<div className="takeaway-box">
<div className="label">Try this for one cycle</div>

For your next cycle, note your discharge type each day in Marea: dry / sticky / creamy / egg-white / watery. Do this without trying to interpret it. By the end of the cycle, you will have mapped your personal mucus pattern — which may differ in timing from textbook descriptions but will be internally consistent across your subsequent cycles.
