Premenstrual chocolate cravings are so common that they have become a cultural shorthand for the experience of PMS. But the reason behind the craving is specific and physiological, not a psychological weakness or lack of willpower. Understanding it changes how you relate to the craving, and what you choose to do about it.

### The calorie story: your metabolism is different in the luteal phase

Progesterone, the dominant hormone in the second half of the cycle, raises resting metabolic rate. Research has found that women burn approximately 100–300 more calories per day in the luteal phase compared to the follicular phase. The body is genuinely expending more energy, and appetite increases in response.

This caloric increase, if unmet, contributes to fatigue, irritability, and intensified cravings. The body is signalling a real energy need, not an imagined one. Eating a bit more in the luteal phase, rather than trying to maintain the same intake as the follicular phase, is physiologically appropriate.

### The serotonin story: why carbohydrates specifically

In the late luteal phase, estrogen falls significantly from its mid-cycle peak. Estrogen supports serotonin synthesis and receptor sensitivity. As estrogen drops, serotonin activity decreases, contributing to the low mood, irritability, and anxiety that many people experience in the premenstrual window.

The body has a mechanism for acutely boosting serotonin through diet: carbohydrates raise blood glucose, triggering insulin release, which clears competing amino acids from the bloodstream and allows tryptophan (serotonin's precursor) to cross the blood-brain barrier more readily. This is why carbohydrate cravings are so specific and targeted in the late luteal phase, the brain is effectively seeking a serotonin boost through the available dietary mechanism.

Chocolate satisfies both the caloric demand (fat and carbohydrate) and, to some extent, the serotonin pathway.

### The magnesium angle

Magnesium is involved in prostaglandin metabolism, and levels are depleted in the premenstrual phase as prostaglandin production ramps up. Magnesium deficiency is associated with worsened PMS symptoms, including mood changes and physical discomfort.

Dark chocolate is a meaningful source of magnesium, a 40g portion of 70%+ dark chocolate provides roughly 65mg, approximately 15–20% of the daily requirement. This may partly explain why the craving is specifically for chocolate rather than another carbohydrate-dense food.

### What this means practically

Trying to override premenstrual food cravings with restriction tends not to work well and can worsen mood and irritability by further depleting available serotonin. A more evidence-aligned approach is to meet the legitimate caloric need, slightly more food overall in the luteal phase, while leaning into lower-glycaemic carbohydrates for more sustained serotonin support: oats, whole grains, legumes.

If chocolate is what the body is signalling for, dark chocolate (70%+) provides the magnesium benefit alongside the carbohydrate-serotonin pathway, without the blood sugar spike and crash of highly processed alternatives.

<div className="label">Research finding</div>

A controlled feeding study measuring food intake across cycle phases found significantly higher spontaneous caloric intake in the luteal phase, averaging 190 calories more per day than the follicular phase. Carbohydrate and fat intake both increased, with the increase most pronounced in the premenstrual week. Participants who ate to appetite rather than restricting reported better mood scores in the late luteal phase than those who attempted to maintain consistent intake.
