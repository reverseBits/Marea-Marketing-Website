When people think about their menstrual cycle, they often think about their period. The bleeding. The few days each month when the cycle makes itself known. But menstruation is one phase of four, and in terms of what your body is doing hormonally, it is not even close to the most active.

### Phase 1: Menstrual (days 1–5 on average)

Your period marks day 1 of your cycle by convention. During menstruation, the uterine lining that built up during the previous cycle sheds, driven by the fall in progesterone and estrogen that triggered it.

Hormonally, this is a low point for both estrogen and progesterone. FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) has begun to rise, beginning the process of recruiting the next group of follicles in the ovaries. Energy levels tend to be lower, and the inflammatory response associated with prostaglandins (which drive the shedding) can affect the whole body — contributing to cramping, fatigue, and sometimes headaches.

This is a phase many people try to push through at full pace. The evidence suggests that acknowledging the body's genuine lower energy state produces better outcomes across the rest of the cycle.

### Phase 2: Follicular (days 1–13 approximately)

The follicular phase overlaps with menstruation at the start and continues after it ends. During this phase, FSH stimulates the development of follicles in the ovaries, each containing an egg. Typically, one follicle becomes dominant.

As follicles develop, they produce estrogen. Estrogen levels rise steadily through the follicular phase, and this has wide-ranging effects. Estrogen supports serotonin activity, which tends to elevate mood and motivation. It supports cognitive clarity — verbal fluency and working memory both show measurable improvements in the follicular phase in research. It also stimulates collagen production, which is why skin can look and feel better in the mid-follicular phase.

Physical energy and strength also tend to peak in the mid-to-late follicular phase, as estrogen supports muscle protein synthesis and is protective against injury.

### Phase 3: Ovulatory (around days 13–15)

Ovulation is triggered by a dramatic surge in LH (luteinising hormone), which causes the dominant follicle to rupture and release an egg. This phase lasts only about 24–48 hours.

The hormonal environment around ovulation is distinctive: estrogen peaks just before the LH surge, creating the highest energy and mood point in the cycle for many people. Libido typically increases, which is biologically logical — ovulation is the only time conception is possible.

Some people feel ovulation physically — a sensation called mittelschmerz, a mild ache on one side of the lower abdomen — though many feel nothing. The egg is viable for 12–24 hours after release. Because sperm can survive in the fallopian tubes for up to five days, the fertile window is roughly days 10–15 for a typical 28-day cycle.

### Phase 4: Luteal (days 15–28 approximately)

After the follicle releases its egg, it transforms into the corpus luteum, a temporary glandular structure that produces progesterone. Progesterone is the defining hormone of the luteal phase.

Progesterone's effects are different from estrogen's. It raises body temperature slightly (which is measurable), has a calming effect on the nervous system via GABA receptors, and prepares the uterine lining for potential implantation. Energy expenditure increases modestly — the body burns slightly more calories in the luteal phase, which partly explains the increase in food cravings.

If no fertilisation occurs, the corpus luteum breaks down, progesterone and estrogen fall, and the uterine lining begins to shed. The sharp drop in progesterone in the late luteal phase is what drives the premenstrual symptom cluster many people experience: disrupted sleep, mood changes, bloating, breast tenderness.

The luteal phase is the most predictable of the four phases — it is almost always 12–14 days, regardless of cycle length. If your cycle varies in length, the variation occurs in the follicular phase.

FSH rises in the follicular phase, peaks briefly before ovulation, then falls. Estrogen rises through the follicular phase, peaks at ovulation, drops briefly, then has a secondary rise in the luteal phase before falling. LH surges sharply at ovulation. Progesterone is nearly absent until after ovulation, then rises steeply through the luteal phase before dropping to near-zero just before menstruation begins.

<div className="label">What to notice this cycle</div>

Pick one thing to track beyond bleeding — energy, mood, skin quality, or sleep. Note it daily for one cycle, including the cycle day. Most people see a clear pattern emerge within a single cycle. Awareness of which phase you are in changes how you interpret what you are feeling.
