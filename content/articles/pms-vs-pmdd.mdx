Premenstrual syndrome is so commonly referenced that it has become shorthand for any mood or physical discomfort before a period. But there is a meaningful clinical distinction between PMS and premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD), and collapsing them into the same category leaves many people without an accurate understanding of what they are experiencing, or what treatment options exist.

### What PMS actually is

PMS refers to a cluster of physical and emotional symptoms that appear in the luteal phase, the second half of the cycle, and resolve within a day or two of menstruation starting. The symptom list is long: bloating, breast tenderness, fatigue, food cravings, mood changes, irritability, difficulty concentrating. By some definitions, up to 75% of people who menstruate experience at least mild premenstrual symptoms.

Most PMS symptoms are real, cyclical, and traceable to the hormonal changes of the luteal phase, but they do not typically prevent normal functioning. Someone can feel bloated, a bit irritable, and tired before their period and still go to work, maintain relationships, and manage daily tasks without significant impairment.

### What PMDD is

PMDD sits in a different category. It is a clinically recognised disorder, listed in the DSM-5, the psychiatric diagnostic manual, characterised by severe mood symptoms in the late luteal phase that cause marked functional impairment. The symptoms must be severe enough to significantly interfere with work, relationships, or daily activities to qualify as PMDD.

The mood symptoms of PMDD are specific: marked emotional lability (sudden sadness, crying, or sensitivity to rejection), marked irritability or anger, marked depressed mood (including feelings of hopelessness), and marked anxiety or tension. Crucially, at least one of these mood symptoms must be present, and they must resolve shortly after menstruation begins. This cyclical pattern is what distinguishes PMDD from a persistent mood disorder.

Physical symptoms, bloating, joint pain, breast tenderness, may also be present, but the diagnosis centres on the severity and functional impact of the mood component.

### The neurobiological mechanism

PMDD is not simply "worse PMS." Research has identified that people with PMDD show an atypical neurological sensitivity to normal hormonal fluctuations. The issue is not that progesterone or estrogen levels are abnormally high or low, in most cases they are not. The issue is that the brain reacts differently to the normal drop in these hormones at the end of the luteal phase.

Specifically, a 2017 study published in *Molecular Psychiatry* found that PMDD involves altered expression of genes in the ESC/E(Z) complex, which regulates how cells respond to hormone fluctuations. This affects the brain's sensitivity to neurosteroids, particularly allopregnanolone, a progesterone metabolite that acts on GABA receptors. In PMDD, the brain's GABA system responds atypically to the luteal-phase rise and fall of this neurosteroid, which explains the severe anxiety, mood dysregulation, and sleep disruption.

This is a neurobiological condition, not a character flaw or a failure to cope.

### Diagnosis and what to bring to a doctor

PMDD is diagnosed through prospective symptom tracking, meaning you record your symptoms daily across at least two full cycles, noting their timing relative to your period. This is important because retrospective recall (trying to remember your symptoms from memory) is often inaccurate. Tracking in Marea across two cycles gives you the kind of time-stamped record that a GP or gynaecologist can use.

PMDD has effective treatments, including SSRIs (which can be taken only in the luteal phase rather than continuously), certain hormonal approaches that stabilise the hormonal environment, and cognitive behavioural therapy for PMDD specifically. The first step is accurate documentation.

<div className="label">What to do</div>

If your premenstrual symptoms are severe enough to affect your work, relationships, or daily functioning in the week before your period, track them daily for two cycles, noting severity on a 1–10 scale and exactly when they appear and disappear. Take this record to your doctor. The cyclical pattern is the key diagnostic evidence.
