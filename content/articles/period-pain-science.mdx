Period pain is one of the most common conditions affecting people who menstruate, and one of the most routinely dismissed. Understanding what is physically happening during menstrual pain is worth knowing, both for the sake of accuracy and because it informs which interventions actually work.

### The prostaglandin mechanism

As the uterine lining prepares to shed, cells in the endometrium produce prostaglandins, lipid compounds that have several effects on the body. In the context of menstruation, prostaglandins trigger two key responses: they cause the uterine muscle to contract, and they cause the blood vessels supplying the uterine muscle to constrict.

The contractions are what physically expel the shed lining. They are strong, research has measured intrauterine pressure during dysmenorrhoea that exceeds that of contractions during labour in some cases. The blood vessel constriction means the contracting muscle receives less oxygen than it needs, creating a state called ischaemia. Ischaemic pain in a repeatedly contracting muscle is the mechanism behind the characteristic cramping sensation.

This is the same physiological mechanism as a muscle cramp anywhere else in the body, a contracting muscle deprived of adequate blood flow. It simply happens in an internal organ you cannot stretch or massage.

### Why some people have more severe pain

People who experience more severe primary dysmenorrhoea (pain with no identifiable underlying cause) typically produce higher concentrations of prostaglandins. This can be measured, prostaglandin levels in menstrual fluid correlate significantly with pain severity.

Prostaglandins also enter the bloodstream, which is why severe menstrual pain is sometimes accompanied by systemic symptoms: nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, headaches, and fainting. These are not coincidental or psychosomatic. They are direct effects of prostaglandins acting on smooth muscle throughout the body.

### Primary vs secondary dysmenorrhoea

Primary dysmenorrhoea is menstrual pain without an identifiable underlying condition. It is the most common type and is caused by the prostaglandin mechanism described above. It typically begins within a couple of years of the first period and may improve with age.

Secondary dysmenorrhoea is menstrual pain caused by an underlying gynaecological condition, most commonly endometriosis, adenomyosis, or uterine fibroids. Pain in secondary dysmenorrhoea may be more severe, may begin before menstruation, may continue after it ends, or may be associated with pain at other times in the cycle or during sex.

The clinical rule of thumb: period pain severe enough to prevent normal functioning, that does not respond adequately to over-the-counter anti-inflammatory medication, or that has changed in character over time, should be investigated. Endometriosis, in particular, is systematically underdiagnosed, average diagnosis time is seven to ten years from symptom onset.

### Why NSAIDs work better than paracetamol

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), ibuprofen, naproxen, work for period pain because they inhibit the enzyme (COX) that produces prostaglandins. Taking an NSAID reduces prostaglandin production, which reduces both the intensity of uterine contractions and the blood vessel constriction. Starting an NSAID before pain peaks, at the first sign of cramping or even the day before a period is due, produces better pain control than taking it after pain is already severe.

Paracetamol has no meaningful effect on prostaglandin production and is consistently less effective for menstrual pain than NSAIDs in clinical trials.

<div className="science-box">
<div className="label">Research finding</div>

A systematic review of 80 randomised trials found NSAIDs significantly more effective than placebo for primary dysmenorrhoea, with a number needed to treat of approximately 2.1, meaning roughly one in two people who take an NSAID for period pain gets meaningful relief that they would not have had with placebo. Starting NSAIDs prophylactically (before pain begins) consistently outperformed reactive dosing.
