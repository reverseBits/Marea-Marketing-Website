Your period is not one event. It is a sequence. And one of the clearest signals within that sequence is colour — which shifts from start to finish in ways that carry real information about what is happening inside your body.

### Why colour changes at all

Period blood is not pure blood. It is a combination of blood, shed uterine lining, cervical mucus, and vaginal secretions. The colour you see depends heavily on how long each component has been in contact with oxygen — a process called oxidation. Fresh blood exposed to minimal oxygen appears bright red. Blood that has pooled and spent more time in the uterine cavity before leaving oxidises into darker shades of red, brown, or near-black.

The speed of your flow is what controls this. On heavier days, blood moves through quickly and stays bright. On lighter days, slower flow gives blood more time to oxidise before leaving the body. This is why most people see brighter red in the middle of their period and darker shades at the beginning or end.

### The full colour spectrum

**Bright red:** Fresh, fast-moving blood. Common on days 2 and 3 for most people. Normal and expected on heavier flow days.

**Dark red or maroon:** Slightly older blood moving more slowly. Typical at the very start of a period when flow has not yet reached its heaviest, or on lighter final days.

**Brown or rust:** Oxidised blood that has spent longer in the uterine cavity. Very common at the beginning and end of a period, and in light spotting before a period starts. Not a sign of anything wrong.

**Light pink or pale rose:** Diluted blood. This appears when flow is very light and blood mixes with a higher proportion of cervical mucus. Common at the very tail end, in the first hour of spotting, or mid-cycle if ovulation bleeding occurs.

**Orange-tinged:** Can appear similar to light brown but with a more yellow-orange quality. Occasionally normal at the very start of a period, but orange blood combined with an unusual odour may warrant a check for bacterial vaginosis or infection.

### Clots — when they are normal and when they are not

Small clots, roughly the size of a coin or smaller, are a normal part of heavy flow days. The uterus produces natural anticoagulants to prevent clotting, but when flow is very heavy, blood can move faster than those anticoagulants can work. The result is small clots, which are physiology doing its job rather than a problem.

Larger clots — consistently bigger than a 10p coin, or appearing on multiple days per period — are worth documenting and discussing with a doctor. Conditions including uterine fibroids, adenomyosis, and hormonal imbalances can produce heavier flow with significant clotting.

### Colours worth flagging

Grey or greyish discharge combined with blood can indicate bacterial vaginosis or infection. If this appears alongside an unusual smell, a healthcare appointment is appropriate. Bright red bleeding that continues beyond eight days, or that saturates a pad within an hour consistently, should also be mentioned to a doctor — not because it is always serious, but because heavy periods can silently cause iron deficiency anaemia over time without obvious warning.
