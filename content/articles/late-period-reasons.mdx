A late period triggers a specific kind of anxiety that is almost universal. And while pregnancy is always worth ruling out, the vast majority of late periods have other explanations — many of them mundane, some worth following up. Understanding the mechanism behind each one helps make sense of your cycle rather than just waiting and hoping.

### 1. Psychological stress

This is the most common non-pregnancy cause of a late period, and the biology behind it is specific. Psychological stress activates the HPA axis, increasing cortisol. Elevated cortisol directly suppresses the hypothalamus's release of GnRH, the hormone that drives the entire chain of ovarian hormone production. If GnRH pulses are disrupted, the LH surge that triggers ovulation is delayed or doesn't occur.

If ovulation is delayed, your period arrives later — because the luteal phase (which follows ovulation) is almost always a fixed 12–14 days. A period that arrives five days late usually means ovulation was five days late, not that the luteal phase changed.

### 2. Significant undereating or caloric restriction

The hypothalamus monitors the body's energy state continuously. When caloric intake drops below what is needed to sustain basic functions, the hypothalamus down-regulates GnRH pulses as a protective mechanism — reproduction is energetically expensive, and the body deprioritises it under scarcity.

This can happen with deliberate restriction (dieting) or unintentional undereating (a very busy period where meals are skipped). Cycle disruption typically appears within one to three cycles of sustained restriction.

### 3. Over-exercise or dramatic increase in training

Intense exercise raises cortisol, creates caloric deficit, and can directly suppress ovarian hormone production. Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) is the term for the syndrome in which training load exceeds energy intake, causing menstrual disruption alongside reduced bone density, impaired immunity, and fatigue. Elite athletes and those who have suddenly increased training volume are at highest risk.

### 4. Thyroid dysfunction

The thyroid governs metabolic rate and interacts directly with the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis. Both hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) and hyperthyroidism (overactive) can disrupt menstrual regularity. Thyroid dysfunction is common — approximately 1 in 8 women will develop a thyroid condition in their lifetime — and is often diagnosed late because symptoms are attributed to other causes.

### 5. Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)

PCOS is the most common hormonal disorder in people of reproductive age, affecting 8–13% of the population. One of its hallmarks is irregular or absent ovulation, which produces irregular cycles. PCOS involves elevated androgens (male hormones), disrupted insulin signalling, and characteristic ovarian appearance on ultrasound. Not everyone with PCOS has all three features, and diagnosis requires clinical assessment.

### 6. Significant illness

Acute illness — a viral infection, a high fever — can delay ovulation by disrupting the hormonal cascade at the hypothalamic level. A period that arrives late following a significant illness is usually a one-off, with the cycle returning to normal the following month.

### 7. Recent time zone changes or major disruption to sleep schedule

The circadian system regulates the timing of hormone pulses throughout the day. Significant disruption to the sleep-wake cycle — jet lag, shift work, sustained sleep deprivation — can alter GnRH pulse timing and delay ovulation.

### 8. Coming off hormonal contraception

Hormonal contraception suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis. After stopping, the axis needs time to reactivate. Cycles may be irregular for several months while the system restores its natural rhythm. This is normal and typically resolves within two to four cycles, though it can take longer.

<div className="label">Research finding</div>

A systematic review of 41 studies examining psychological stress and menstrual function found that 88.5% of studies reported a significant association between the two. Reported effects included delayed ovulation, skipped cycles, and shortened luteal phases. The relationship was consistent across diverse populations and not explained by confounding lifestyle variables — confirming psychological stress as a direct cause of cycle disruption.

<div className="takeaway-box">
<div className="label">When to see a doctor</div>

A single late period after an identifiable stressor usually does not require investigation. If your cycles are consistently longer than 35 days, if you regularly skip periods without a clear cause, or if you experience other symptoms (unexplained weight changes, excess hair growth, significant fatigue), a GP appointment with a cycle log is the appropriate next step.
